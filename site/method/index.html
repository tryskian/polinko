Research method

Polinko turns behavioural questions into source-traceable evidence and bounded claims.

I lead the research and work with AI collaborators to develop questions, build experiments, test assumptions, and inspect the results. Source artefacts stay connected to each judgement so the evidence remains visible before I promote a public claim.

Working together

The process is collaborative from question to implementation.

My role

I define the research question, behavioural boundary, source material, acceptance criteria, and evidence conditions. I also decide how the result should be interpreted and whether it can support a claim.

AI collaborators

AI systems support dialogue, synthesis, reframing, stress-testing, implementation, validation, and documentation. They also produce the behaviour Polinko evaluates.

AI output is working material inside the method. Source authority, evidence selection, research judgement, and final authorship remain mine.

Source-first evidence

Claims stay connected to the material that produced them.

Source artefact

A question may begin in a transcript, chat, notebook, database, runtime trace, eval report, screenshot, or direct interaction. The relevant source stays identifiable throughout the work.

Human judgement

I judge a row or case against its stated boundary, record exclusions, and decide what belongs in the evidence set. A summary cannot replace the source material that supports it.

Pre-Beta 2.4 keeps source artefacts and row or case judgements visible before evidence is summarised at lane level.

From question to claim

Each stage narrows the distance between an observation and a claim.

The process can loop as the evidence changes the question, the case, or the implementation. The order still matters because interpretation should not erase the operational result that came before it.

  1. 01

    Define one behavioural question, its source material, and the exact boundary the system is expected to hold.

  2. 02

    Work with AI collaborators to reframe, stress-test, and translate the question into an inspectable experiment or evaluation surface.

  3. 03

    Implement the test while preserving the source interaction, constraints, context, and corrections that shaped it.

  4. 04

    Judge each row or case against its stated boundary and record the first operational result before adding a broader interpretation.

  5. 05

    Retain in-scope failures as evidence, remove invalid cases with an explicit reason, and rerun only when the evidence boundary allows it.

  6. 06

    Compare repeated evidence at lane level, then decide whether it supports a bounded conclusion, a revised question, or no claim yet.

Source
The interaction, artefact, report, or record from which the research question and evidence are drawn.
Judgement
A human decision about whether a row or case held its boundary, including exclusions and failure disposition.
Lane signal
A repeated behavioural shape supported by multiple inspectable rows or cases within one research lane.
Claim
A bounded interpretation made only when the lane signal remains traceable to its judgements and source material.
Binary judgement

The first case judgement stays binary and visible.

Polinko records pass or fail first because a binary gate aligns the evaluation with binary computation. When a case fails, retain or evict records whether the failure belongs in the evidence set.

Pass

The system held the exact behavioural boundary being tested in that row or case.

Fail

The system crossed, weakened, or contradicted the stated boundary. The failure is then retained as evidence or evicted as an invalid case.

Source record

Each judgement stays attached to the evidence that produced it.

  • The research question and behavioural boundary remain named.
  • The source interaction or artefact remains identifiable.
  • The first result remains exactly pass or fail.
  • Retained failures and evicted cases keep their reasons.
  • Interpretation is added after the operational judgement.
  • One row or case cannot become a lane-level claim by itself.

Authorship and responsibility

The collaboration is visible, and the final judgement is mine.

I created and lead Polinko, contribute directly to its engineering, and remain responsible for its questions, evidence boundaries, interpretations, and claims. AI collaboration is part of the method, but it does not replace source authority or human judgement.

Human responsibility

I define what the research is testing, what counts as evidence, and what can enter the public record.

AI contribution

AI systems support dialogue, synthesis, reframing, implementation, validation, stress-testing, and documentation. They also produce observable behaviour inside the experiments.

Final boundary

I select the evidence, interpret the results, authorise the claim, and make the final publication decision.
